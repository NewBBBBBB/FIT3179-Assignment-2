Economic Overview of Malaysia

Real GDP of Malaysia

Real Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is a measure of a nation's
economic output, adjusted for inflation. Unlike nominal GDP,
Real GDP takes into account price changes, providing a more
accurate reflection of a country's economic performance over
time.

A notable sharp decline is observed in 2020 due to the economic
impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, followed by a gradual recovery
in the subsequent years. The recovery seen in 2021 onwards
highlights the resilience of the Malaysian economy as it adjusted
to new norms.

Breakdown of GDP by Sector in Malaysia

Malaysia's GDP is driven by multiple sectors, including agriculture,
construction, manufacturing, mining and quarrying, and services. Each
sector plays a significant role in shaping the country's economic landscape.
Among them, the services sector has consistently been the largest contributor to the nation's GDP.

FDI Flows into Malaysia

Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) into Malaysia refers to the investment made by a company or individual
in one country into business interests located in another country. The overall trend of FDI inflows is positive, with a significant increase from 2016 to 2023. However, there is a notable dip in 2020, likely due to the COVID-19 pandemic.

Unemployment in Malaysia (2022)

Unemployment rate is a measure of the percentage of the labor force that is without a job but is actively seeking employment. The unemployment rate serves as a key indicator of the health of a country's economy. Sabah in Malaysia has the highest unemployment rate in 2022 among all states in Malaysia, meanwhile, Putrajaya has the lowest unemployment rate in 2022 among all states in Malaysia.

Unemployment vs Poverty in Malaysia

Unemployment Rate represents the proportion of the labor force that is without a job but actively seeking employment. A higher unemployment rate suggests a weaker economy with fewer job opportunities. Absolute Poverty represents a household is classified as being in absolute poverty if its gross monthly income falls below the Poverty Line Income (PLI), meaning the household lacks sufficient income to cover basic necessities for a dignified life. There is a generally positive correlation between unemployment and poverty rates. This means that as unemployment rates increase, poverty rates tend to increase as well.
