Economic Overview of Malaysia

Real GDP of Malaysia

Real Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is a measure of a
nation's economic output, adjusted for inflation. Unlike
nominal GDP, Real GDP takes into account price changes,
providing a more accurate reflection of a country's economic
performance over time.

A notable sharp decline is observed in 2020 due to the
economic impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, followed by a
gradual recovery in the subsequent years.

The recovery seen in 2021 onwards highlights the resilience of
the Malaysian economy as it adjusted to new norms.

Breakdown of GDP by Sector in Malaysia

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) by Sector provides insights
into the contribution of different sectors to a country's
economy. The data is typically categorized into sectors such
as agriculture, manufacturing, services, and construction.

Among them, the services sector has consistently been
the largest contributor to the nation's GDP.

Import duties sector has been the lowest contributor
throughout the years.

FDI Flows into Malaysia

Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) into Malaysia refers to
the investment made by companies or individuals in one
country into business interests located in another country.
FDI is an important driver of economic growth.

The overall trend of FDI inflows is positive, with a
significant increase from 2016 to 2023.

However, there is a notable dip in 2020, likely due to the
COVID-19 pandemic.

Unemployment in Malaysia (2022)

Unemployment Rate in Malaysia is a key economic
indicator that measures the percentage of the labor force
that is without a job but actively seeking employment.
A high unemployment rate can indicate economic distress.

In 2022, the state of Sabah in East Malaysia had the highest
unemployment rate among all states in the country.

Putrajaya has the lowest unemployment rate in 2022 among
all states in Malaysia.

Unemployment vs Poverty in Malaysia

Unemployment Rate represents the proportion of the labor force that is without a job but actively seeking employment. A higher unemployment rate suggests a weaker economy with fewer job opportunities. Absolute Poverty represents a household is classified as being in absolute poverty if its gross monthly income falls below the Poverty Line Income (PLI), meaning the household lacks sufficient income to cover basic necessities for a dignified life. There is a generally positive correlation between unemployment and poverty rates. This means that as unemployment rates increase, poverty rates tend to increase as well.
