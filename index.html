Economic Overview of Malaysia

Real GDP of Malaysia

Real Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is a
measure of a nation's economic output, adjusted for
inflation. Unlike nominal GDP, Real GDP takes into
account price changes, providing a more accurate
reflection of a country's economic performance over time.
A higher GDP often reflects increased economic health.

A notable sharp decline is observed in 2020 due to the
economic impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, followed
by a gradual recovery in the subsequent years.

The recovery seen in 2021 onwards highlights the
resilience of the Malaysian economy as it adjusted to new
norms.

Breakdown of GDP by Sectors in Malaysia

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) by
Sectors provides insights into the contribution of
different sectors to a country's economy. The data is
typically categorized into sectors such as agriculture,
manufacturing, services, and construction.

Among them, the Services sector has consistently been
the largest contributor to the nation's GDP.

Import duties sector has been the lowest contributor
throughout the years.

FDI Flows into Malaysia

Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) into
Malaysia refers to the investment made by companies or
individuals in one country into business interests located
in another country. A healthy FDI inflow suggests
strong economic growth prospects.

The overall trend of net FDI flows is positive, with a
significant increase from 2016 to 2023.

However, there is a notable dip in 2020, likely due to
the COVID-19 pandemic.

Unemployment Rate in Malaysia (2022)

Unemployment Rate in Malaysia is a key
economic indicator that measures the percentage of the
labor force that is without a job but actively seeking
employment. A higher unemployment rate can
indicate economic distress.

In 2022, the state of Sabah in East Malaysia had the
highest unemployment rate among all states in the
country.

Putrajaya has the lowest unemployment rate in 2022
among all states in Malaysia.

Unemployment vs Poverty in Malaysia

Unemployment Rate vs Poverty Rate
in Malaysia shows the relationship between the
unemployment rate and the poverty rate in the country.
A higher unemployment rate is often associated
with higher poverty rate.

There is a positive correlation between unemployment
and poverty rates in Malaysia. As unemployment rate
increases, poverty rate tends to increase as well.

Cost of Living Index

Cost of Living Index (COLI) measures the
relative cost of living across different locations by
quantifying expenses for essential goods and
services. A higher COLI indicates a more
expensive living environment.

In 2018, Malaysia ranked 4, indicating a moderate cost
of living. The rank dipped to 5 in 2020 and 6 in 2022,
suggesting that the relative cost of living has become
more affordable.
